ðŸ”µ Central Overview
Phylogeny = evolutionary history; systematics integrates classification and evolutionary relationships using homologies, cladistics, and molecular evidence.
Full explanation (expand/collapse)
The evolutionary history of a species or group of species is called phylogeny. The fossil record provides a chronicle of evolutionary change that helps trace phylogeny but is incomplete. Biologists combine fossil evidence with morphological, developmental, and molecular data to build hypotheses of evolutionary relationships. Distinguishing homology (similarity due to common ancestry) from analogy (convergent evolution) is essential. These hypotheses are depicted as phylogenetic treesâ€”branching diagrams representing descent hypotheses, which are revised as new data are collected.
ðŸŸ  15.14 â€” Phylogenies based on homologies reflect evolutionary history
Short: Homologous structures and molecular sequences provide evidence of common ancestry; analogous traits arise through convergent evolution.
Full explanation (expand/collapse)
Homologies are similarities due to shared ancestry. Homologous structures may look or function differently but retain fundamental structural similarity because they evolved from the same ancestral structure. Analogy results from convergent evolution, where unrelated lineages independently evolve similar features because of similar selective pressures. Distinguishing homology from analogy is critical in systematics. Example: The Australian "mole" (a marsupial) and the North American mole (a eutherian) show similar digging adaptations (enlarged forepaws, reduced eyes), yet their reproductive strategies differ (marsupial pouch vs. placental gestation). Genetic and fossil evidence indicate their last common ancestor lived ~140 million years ago and was not mole-like â€” hence these similarities are analogous (Figure 15.14).
Figure 15.14: Australian "mole" (marsupial) vs North American mole (eutherian) â€” analogous burrowing adaptations; last common ancestor ~140 MYA.
ðŸ”µ 15.15 â€” Systematics connects classification with evolutionary history
Short: Systematics (taxonomy + phylogeny) aims to classify organisms to reflect evolutionary relationships; Linnaean names persist.
Full explanation (expand/collapse)
Systematics is the discipline that classifies organisms and determines evolutionary relationships. Taxonomy (naming) is part of systematics. Each species has a two-part (binomial) Latinized name (genus + specific epithet), e.g., Felis catus. Linnaean ranks: species â†’ genus â†’ family â†’ order â†’ class â†’ phylum â†’ kingdom â†’ domain. Each named group is a taxon (plural: taxa). Modern approaches aim to make classification reflect phylogeny, favoring monophyletic groups (include common ancestor and all descendants). The PhyloCode is an approach that names strictly monophyletic groups without ranks. ðŸ”´ 15.16 â€” Shared characters are used to construct phylogenetic trees
Short: Cladistics groups organisms into clades using shared derived characters; outgroup comparison polarizes characters; parsimony selects simplest trees.
Full explanation (expand/collapse)
Cladistics groups organisms by common ancestry into clades (a clade is monophyletic). Cladistics distinguishes shared ancestral characters (characters inherited from an earlier ancestor) from shared derived characters (novel traits that define a clade). To infer phylogeny, systematists score taxa for characters (presence=1, absence=0) and use an outgroup (a lineage known to have diverged earlier) to determine polarity (ancestral vs derived). Parsimony favors trees requiring the fewest character changes. Example (Figure 15.16A): taxa = frog (outgroup), iguana, duck-billed platypus, kangaroo, beaver. Characters include amnion, hair+mammary glands, gestation, long gestation. The platypus (egg-laying mammal) branches early among mammals; kangaroo shows short gestation (pouch), beaver long gestation typical of placentals. Trees are hypotheses: molecular and morphological data sometimes redraw relationships. Example (Figure 15.16B): birds nest within reptiles (dinosaur clade), making "Reptilia" excluding birds non-monophyletic. Fossil evidence like Oviraptor fossils (Fig. 15.16C) supports predictions (e.g., brooding behavior).
Figure 15.16A: Character matrix â†’ cladogram (frog outgroup; platypus branches early). Figure 15.16B: Reptileâ€“bird phylogeny (birds nested within dinosaurs). Figure 15.16C: Oviraptor fossil with eggs suggests brooding.
ðŸŸ¢ 15.17 â€” An organism's evolutionary history is documented in its genome
Short: Molecular systematics uses DNA/RNA/proteins to infer relationships; different genes are useful at different time scales (e.g., rRNA slow, mtDNA fast).
Full explanation (expand/collapse)
Molecular sequences accumulate changes over time; closely related species have more similar sequences. Molecular systematics leverages DNA, RNA, and protein data to reconstruct phylogenies spanning deep and recent divergences. Example: the red panda â€” previously linked to giant pandas or raccoons by morphology â€” molecular data suggest it forms its own lineage closer to raccoons/weasels. Figure 15.17 shows a molecular phylogeny of bears, raccoons, weasels, and the red panda with an approximate timeline. Genome comparisons reveal wide homology: ~99% of human genes have homologs in mice; ~50% with yeast. Gene duplication fuels innovation. Gene number alone doesnâ€™t explain complexity â€” regulatory elements, alternative splicing, and gene versatility matter.

ðŸŸ£ 15.18 â€” Molecular clocks help track evolutionary time
Short: Molecular clocks estimate divergence times from sequence differences calibrated against fossils; clocks can be noisy and are improved with many genes.
Full explanation (expand/collapse)
Some gene regions change at approximately steady rates; by comparing sequence divergence to fossil-calibrated dates, researchers can estimate divergence times (molecular clocks). Example: HIV molecular clock â€” sequence data including a 1959 sample reveal clocklike evolution; extrapolation dates origin of widespread HIV-1 M to ~1930s (Fig. 15.18). Clock reliability varies among genes, taxa, and times. Multigene approaches reduce error. Large analyses using many genes have produced divergence estimates consistent with fossil records but remain uncertain for very ancient events (e.g., >1 billion years) because of rate variation and calibration limits.
Figure 15.18: Plot of HIV sequence differences vs sampling year; line used to estimate earliest human infection (~1930s).
ðŸŸ¡ 15.19 â€” Constructing the tree of life is a work in progress
Short: Modern classification recognizes three domains (Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya); extensive early horizontal gene exchange suggests network or ring models alongside tree models.
Full explanation (expand/collapse)
Phylogenetic trees are hypotheses and are revised. Classification evolved from two kingdoms to five kingdoms and now three domains: Bacteria, Archaea, Eukarya. rRNA phylogenies suggest bacteria diverged first, with archaea closer to eukaryotes. Comparisons of whole genomes reveal early horizontal gene transfer (HGT) between lineages (via plasmids, viruses, endosymbiosis). Key transfers include genes from mitochondrial and chloroplast ancestors to eukaryotic ancestors (endosymbiotic events). Because of HGT, some propose network/ring models for early life (Figure 15.19B), where eukaryotes may have arisen from fusion of an archaeal and a bacterial lineage.
Figure 15.19A/B: rRNA-based three-domain tree and alternative ring model showing gene fusion/HGT events.
